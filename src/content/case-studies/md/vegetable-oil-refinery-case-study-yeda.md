The Refinery Reset: How Listening Changed Everything

Industry: Food Production
Focus: Vegetable Oil Refining Capacity Expansion Under Constraint



## At a Glance

- Required to double refining capacity while maintaining the existing building footprint

- Internal process knowledge was informal and undocumented

- Early exploration revealed no clear technical solution within constraints

- A returning consultant identified a bypassed heat exchanger as the hidden bottleneck

- Final solution included reactivated process design, selective automation, and early adoption of new deodorization tech—delivering full capacity with margin



## The Situation

At a printing paper manufacturing plant, the refining process — where pulp fibers are mechanically treated to improve bonding and chemical fixation — plays a critical role in determining final paper characteristics. The process involves complex interactions between fiber type, refiner design, and mechanical action.



## The Challenge

There was no obvious failure. But after being asked to benchmark best practices against a professional article, a deeper look revealed that what appeared to be a functioning system was actually a compromised setup. The impact? Millions in unnecessary cost and missed potential.



## The Underlying Theory

Different fiber types require different refining approaches:
- Long fibers (softwood) need a cutting action to improve flexibility.
- Short fibers (hardwood) benefit from gentle defibrillation to preserve integrity.

The refiner disks themselves must be matched to these fiber types. Their tooth design, material properties, and mechanical action are not one-size-fits-all.

Despite having two refining lines originally designed to separate fiber types, over time the system had been modified for capacity. This led to mixed fiber streams, generalized plate designs, and cost-driven material substitutions. The disks were brittle and prone to breakage. Maintenance issues were "solved" by reducing refining intensity, at the cost of refining quality. A textbook case of solving the wrong problem.

## Our Approach

The investigation began with extensive literature review, reaching beyond the article into the referenced research and source books. The deeper I read, the clearer it became: we were doing it wrong.

I contacted the article's author — a senior engineer from an equipment supplier — and initiated a technical discussion. Despite the limited tools of the 1990s (fax machines and shared computers), I shared what I had uncovered and asked for guidance.

Their recommendations:
- Re-establish strict separation between short and long fiber lines
- Install additional refiners to handle capacity without compromise
- Replace the brittle refiner disks with ones engineered to deform under stress rather than fracture

To validate internally, I collected microscopic images of processed fibers to demonstrate structural damage. I consulted in-house experts to win support, and with full backing from my manager, prepared a detailed project plan with ROI projection.

It was my first major industrial initiative. When it was approved, I knew this was a career-defining moment.

## The Results

The transformation unfolded in stages:
- Improved filler retention, increasing yield and reducing material loss
- Enhanced chemical functionality, leading to more effective bonding
- Improved mechanical properties: strength, rigidity, and porosity
- Better runnability, enabling energy reduction for equivalent output

In the long term:
- Filler content increased without compromising product specs
- Long fiber content was reduced from 40% to 20%, significantly lowering costs
- Procurement was empowered to source lower-cost fibers, confident the process could compensate to maintain quality.
